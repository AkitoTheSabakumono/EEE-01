Passage A — The Science of Sleep

For centuries, sleep was considered a passive state — a time when the body simply shut down and rested. Modern neuroscience has completely overturned this assumption. During sleep, particularly during the deep stages, the brain is extraordinarily active. It consolidates memories formed during the day, processes emotional experiences, and clears out toxic proteins that accumulate during waking hours.

Research conducted at the University of Rochester revealed that the brain has a dedicated cleaning system called the glymphatic system, which becomes ten times more active during sleep. This system flushes cerebrospinal fluid through the brain, removing waste products including beta-amyloid, a protein associated with Alzheimer's disease. Scientists now believe that chronic sleep deprivation may be a significant contributing factor to the development of neurodegenerative diseases.

Beyond the physical benefits, adequate sleep profoundly affects cognitive performance. Students who sleep fewer than six hours before an exam consistently perform worse than those who sleep seven to nine hours, regardless of total study time. This suggests that studying until the early hours of the morning — a common practice among exam candidates — may be counterproductive. The brain needs sleep not merely to rest, but to learn.

Passage B — Urban Farming: Growing Up

As cities expand and agricultural land diminishes, a growing number of urban planners and entrepreneurs are looking skyward for solutions. Vertical farming — the practice of growing crops in stacked layers inside controlled indoor environments — has moved from a futuristic concept to a practical reality in cities from Tokyo to New York.

Proponents argue that vertical farms offer significant advantages over conventional agriculture. They use up to 95% less water through recirculation systems, require no pesticides due to the controlled environment, and can produce harvests year-round regardless of weather conditions. A single vertical farm occupying one city block can reportedly match the output of many hectares of traditional farmland.

Critics, however, point to the enormous energy costs involved. Vertical farms rely entirely on artificial lighting, which currently makes them economically viable only for high-value crops such as leafy greens and herbs. Scaling the technology to produce staple crops like wheat or rice remains, for now, economically unfeasible. Nevertheless, as renewable energy costs continue to fall, many experts predict that vertical farming will play an increasingly important role in global food security.
